## Applicable Law

Operators are responsible for understanding and complying with all laws relevant to their jurisdiction and use case. This includes but is not limited to:

**United States**
- Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA), 18 U.S.C. § 1030 — prohibits unauthorized access to computer systems. Accessing a `.onion` service to which you have no authorization may constitute a violation.
- Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA) — governs interception and disclosure of electronic communications.

**United Kingdom**
- Computer Misuse Act 1990 — criminalizes unauthorized access to computer material and unauthorized modification of computer contents.

**European Union / International**
- General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) — if findings contain personal data of EU residents (names, emails, addresses), you are a data controller and must comply with GDPR obligations including data minimization, retention limits, and subject rights.
- California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) — similar obligations for California residents' personal information.

**No jurisdiction is exempt.** Operating this tool does not grant authority to access systems or services you do not own or have documented permission to monitor.

## Handling Discovered Data

Dark web findings frequently contain **personally identifiable information (PII)** — credentials, names, addresses, financial data — belonging to individuals who are themselves victims of a breach or crime.

When this tool surfaces such data:

- **Minimize retention.** Store only what is operationally necessary. Do not retain PII beyond the period required for your investigation or monitoring purpose.
- **Restrict access.** Limit access to findings containing PII to personnel with a documented operational need.
- **Do not re-expose.** Do not republish, share, or transfer discovered PII to unauthorized parties.
- **Follow disclosure obligations.** Depending on your jurisdiction and role, discovering evidence of a breach may trigger mandatory notification obligations.
